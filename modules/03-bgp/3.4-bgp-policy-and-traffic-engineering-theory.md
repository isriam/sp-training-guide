# 3.4 — BGP Policy and Traffic Engineering — Protocol Theory

## Protocol Overview

BGP policy is the real protocol. The wire format, FSM, and UPDATE messages are just the plumbing. What matters operationally is how attributes are manipulated on import and export to influence path selection and traffic flow.

There are two distinct traffic engineering problems in BGP:

- **Outbound traffic engineering**: choosing which path *your AS* uses to leave the network.
- **Inbound traffic engineering**: influencing which path *other ASes* use to enter your network.

Outbound TE is comparatively easy because you control the decision process inside your AS. Inbound TE is the dark art because you're trying to influence remote decision processes you do not control, using hints they may ignore.

BGP policy works by mutating attributes before routes enter the decision process, or before they leave your AS. The protocol itself doesn't care whether a LOCAL_PREF of 500 means "premium transit" or "route from Steve's cursed lab router" — it just compares numbers and obeys the rules.

## Core Mechanisms

### 1. Import vs Export Policy

BGP policy is directional. This is the first thing people mangle.

**Import policy** runs when a route is received from a peer, before or during installation into Adj-RIB-In / Loc-RIB.

Typical import actions:
- accept / reject route
- set LOCAL_PREF
- set communities for internal signaling
- validate RPKI and tag result
- normalize MED, strip dangerous attributes
- apply customer/transit/peer role logic

**Export policy** runs when a route is sent to a peer, after best path selection.

Typical export actions:
- decide whether to advertise route at all
- prepend AS_PATH
- attach provider-specific communities
- strip LOCAL_PREF
- set MED (for neighboring AS)
- suppress more specifics / only advertise aggregates

**Core truth:**
- Import policy controls *your own* path selection.
- Export policy controls *what you tell others*.

Mixing those mentally is how people build elegant disasters.

### 2. Outbound Traffic Engineering

Outbound traffic engineering is about influencing the BGP best-path decision inside your AS.

**Primary tools:**
- LOCAL_PREF
- Weight (vendor-local, usually Cisco only)
- IGP metric to next-hop (hot potato)
- MED (only if comparing paths from same neighbor AS or if always-compare enabled)
- Multipath / ECMP

#### LOCAL_PREF

This is the dominant outbound TE tool in an SP.

**Properties:**
- Higher is better
- Propagated only within iBGP
- Not sent to eBGP peers
- Usually set on ingress from eBGP peers

**Typical design pattern:**
- Customer > peer > transit
- Premium transit provider > backup transit provider
- Regional exit preference based on cost/performance

Example internal policy scale:
- Customer route: 300
- Peer route: 200
- Primary transit: 150
- Backup transit: 100
- Last resort / blackhole / degraded: lower still

The beauty of LOCAL_PREF is that it is deterministic within your AS. You are not negotiating with the Internet. You are issuing orders.

#### Weight

A vendor-local attribute, most famously Cisco-specific.

**Properties:**
- Highest wins
- Local to a single router
- Not propagated to any peer

Useful for per-router preference, but usually a poor foundation for network-wide SP policy because it doesn't scale operationally. Great for emergency steering or edge-specific hacks. Not good as the main architecture unless you enjoy finding landmines in six months.

#### Hot Potato Routing

When all earlier attributes tie, BGP prefers the path with the lowest IGP metric to the BGP next-hop.

This causes traffic to exit the AS at the closest egress point — minimizing internal transit cost. That behavior is called **hot potato routing**.

**Why it matters:**
- IGP metric tuning directly influences BGP best path
- Route reflectors may choose differently than PEs if their IGP viewpoint differs
- TE decisions made only with LOCAL_PREF may still be overridden by hot potato when paths are otherwise equal

**Cold potato** behavior is the opposite — intentionally carry traffic farther inside your network before exiting, usually to control performance or preserve capacity contracts. Cold potato requires policy overrides, not default BGP behavior.

### 3. Inbound Traffic Engineering

Inbound traffic engineering tries to influence how remote ASes choose a path into your network.

You do not control their decision process. You can only place bait on the table and hope the remote policy engine bites in the direction you wanted.

**Primary tools:**
- AS_PATH prepending
- MED (toward directly connected neighboring AS)
- More-specific advertisement / deaggregation
- BGP communities recognized by upstream providers
- Selective advertisement / conditional advertisement

#### AS_PATH Prepending

Artificially lengthen the AS_PATH on export by adding your own AS multiple times.

Example:
- Normal export: `65000`
- Prepended export: `65000 65000 65000`

**Goal:** make the path look longer so remote ASes prefer another path.

**Reality:**
- Works only if remote ASes compare AS_PATH at that stage and don't override with LOCAL_PREF or other policy.
- Mostly useful toward upstreams when trying to make one ingress less attractive.
- Very coarse control — like steering with a shovel.

**Limits:**
- Some networks ignore AS_PATH length or heavily override with policy.
- Excessive prepending can make failover ugly or cause traffic to shift in unexpected ways.

#### MED (Multi-Exit Discriminator)

MED tells a neighboring AS which entry point you prefer they use when they have multiple links to you.

**Properties:**
- Lower is better
- Non-transitive
- Intended for comparison between paths from the same neighboring AS only

**Use case:**
- AS 65000 has two links to AS 65100: East and West
- AS 65000 sets MED 10 on East and MED 50 on West
- AS 65100, if it honors MED, prefers entering via East

**Problem:**
MED handling varies by vendor and policy. Comparing MED across different neighbor ASes can create instability and oscillation. MED is fine within a bilateral relationship. It becomes cursed when treated as a global signal.

#### More-Specific Advertisement

Advertise a more specific prefix on one path to attract traffic there.

Example:
- Advertise aggregate `203.0.113.0/23` from both upstreams
- Advertise `203.0.113.0/24` only from upstream A
- Traffic to that /24 enters via A because longest-match wins, regardless of AS_PATH

**This is powerful and dangerous.**
It overrides most normal BGP path preference because forwarding honors longest prefix match before any BGP attribute comparison.

**Risks:**
- Table pollution (spraying specifics into the DFZ)
- Filter rejection if the prefix is too specific
- Operational fragility during failover

#### Provider Communities

The best inbound TE usually comes from provider-defined communities.

Examples of upstream community actions:
- Lower LOCAL_PREF within provider's network
- Do not advertise to peers
- Prepend toward specific upstreams
- Blackhole traffic in a given region
- Keep route local to a metro/region/continent

This is more precise than blind AS_PATH prepending because you're directly influencing the upstream's internal policy, not hoping they infer your intent from path length.

### 4. Communities in Depth

Communities are policy labels. They let you decouple route tagging from route matching logic.

#### Standard Communities (RFC 1997)

4 bytes. Typically displayed as `ASN:value`.

**Well-known values:**
- `NO_EXPORT` — don't advertise to eBGP peers
- `NO_ADVERTISE` — don't advertise to anyone
- `NO_EXPORT_SUBCONFED` — don't leave local confederation
- `NOPEER` — do not advertise to bilateral peers

**Operational pattern:**
Tag routes on ingress, match tags later in policy. This creates a clean pipeline:
1. classify route
2. attach internal meaning
3. act on that meaning elsewhere

#### Extended Communities (RFC 4360)

8 bytes with typed structure. Commonly used for:
- Route Targets (VPN import/export control)
- Site of Origin (loop prevention in multi-homed VPNs)
- bandwidth signaling
- color / intent signaling in SR policy ecosystems

Extended communities are more structured than standard communities and are essential for VPN and service policy.

#### Large Communities (RFC 8092)

12 bytes, typically `ASN:value1:value2`.

Created because 4-byte ASNs made standard community conventions ugly and insufficient.

**Why operators like them:**
- easy to read
- enough namespace to encode policy cleanly
- not overloaded with VPN semantics like extended communities

Typical pattern:
- First field = operator ASN
- Second field = policy domain / function
- Third field = parameter / action

Example:
- `64512:100:10` = customer route, region 10
- `64512:200:666` = blackhole request
- `64512:300:50` = lower local-pref to 50

### 5. Community-Based Policy Frameworks

The scalable way to run BGP policy is not giant prefix-lists stapled to every neighbor. It's a tag-and-act framework.

**Model:**
1. On ingress, classify route source and intent
2. Attach communities or large communities representing that classification
3. In later policy stages, match on tags rather than raw prefixes or ad hoc conditions
4. Strip or preserve tags intentionally on export

**Example internal taxonomy:**
- `64512:100:x` = route source classification
- `64512:200:x` = local-pref class
- `64512:300:x` = geographic region
- `64512:400:x` = blackhole/security actions
- `64512:500:x` = export control markers

This turns policy from spaghetti into an actual system. A miracle, honestly.

### 6. Route Maps / Policy Chains as Evaluation Pipelines

Every vendor implements policy as ordered evaluation:
- IOS route-map sequence numbers
- Junos policy-statement term order
- XR route-policy if/elseif blocks

**Generic evaluation model:**
1. Check route against match conditions
2. If matched, apply actions (set attribute, accept, reject, continue)
3. If not matched, move to next rule
4. If no rule matches, default action applies (vendor-specific, but often reject in structured policy languages and permit in some legacy ACL-style systems unless bound appropriately)

**Best practice:**
Policy should be explicit about defaults. If the default is "permit anything not matched," eventually something awful walks through the side door.

### 7. Route Flap Damping (Historical and Mostly Regretted)

Route flap damping penalizes routes that change state repeatedly.

**Mechanism:**
- Each flap adds a penalty
- Penalty decays exponentially over time
- If penalty exceeds suppress threshold, route is suppressed
- Once decay drops below reuse threshold, route becomes usable again

This sounded brilliant in the 1990s and then turned out to be too aggressive for modern Internet convergence. It suppressed legitimate routes for too long and amplified instability.

**Modern operator consensus:**
- Avoid classic aggressive damping in the DFZ
- If used at all, use extremely conservative parameters and scope it carefully

Like many old ideas, it was invented by serious people with good intentions and then weaponized by reality.

### 8. Remotely Triggered Black Hole (RTBH)

RTBH uses BGP to signal that traffic to a destination should be discarded upstream, typically for DDoS mitigation.

**Basic model:**
1. Operator injects a host route (or specific prefix) into BGP
2. Route carries a special blackhole community recognized by upstream/provider
3. Upstream sets next-hop to null / blackhole or installs discard route
4. Attack traffic is dropped before it reaches the target network

**Forms:**
- **Destination-based RTBH**: drop traffic TO the attacked destination
- **Source-based RTBH**: drop traffic FROM the attacking source(s), often using uRPF and policy

**Standards:**
RFC 7999 defines the well-known BLACKHOLE community (`65535:666`). Providers may also use their own communities.

**Trade-off:**
You save the network and sacrifice reachability to the attacked host/prefix. That's not elegant, but it's better than letting a 200 Gbps clown parade saturate your edge.

### 9. BGP Multipath / ECMP

BGP can install multiple equal-cost best paths for the same prefix.

**Typical equality requirements:**
- same LOCAL_PREF
- same AS_PATH length
- same ORIGIN
- same MED (where applicable)
- same next-hop resolution class / IGP metric depending on platform and settings
- for iBGP multipath, often same route origin type and same RR-derived properties unless relaxed

**Why multipath matters:**
- load sharing across multiple transits
- load sharing across multiple iBGP exits
- better bandwidth utilization
- supports PIC edge/core convergence models

**Caveat:**
True equality in BGP is often stricter than operators expect. "Looks equal to me" is not a protocol state.

### 10. Conditional Advertisement

Conditional advertisement lets a router advertise one set of routes only if another route exists or does not exist.

**Use cases:**
- advertise backup prefixes only when primary path fails
- selectively leak a more specific during outage conditions
- automate failover announcements without external orchestration

**Conceptual model:**
- if route X is present → advertise Y
- if route X is absent → advertise Z

This is effectively route-based control logic using BGP state as the trigger.

### 11. Policy Oscillation and Stability

BGP policy can create stable routing or self-inflicted chaos.

#### MED oscillation
Classic problem described in RFC 3345. When MED is compared across paths in certain RR topologies, routers can keep changing best path in response to each other's choices, never settling cleanly.

**Mitigations:**
- deterministic MED processing
- compare MED only within same neighboring AS
- reduce RR path-hiding side effects
- use ADD-PATH in some designs

#### Policy feedback loops
If community-based actions at one AS trigger remote policy, and the remote policy sends attributes back that re-trigger local policy, you can build feedback loops.

Example:
- You tag route for low-pref at provider A
- Provider A prepends to peers
- remote network now prefers provider B
- provider B community logic shifts local-pref regionally
- your inbound pattern changes in ways you didn't model

Policy is distributed systems. Distributed systems are where certainty goes to die.

## Protocol Interactions

**Policy ↔ Best Path Algorithm**: Most policy actions exist to modify attributes *before* the decision process. LOCAL_PREF, MED, communities, AS_PATH prepending — these all work because BGP compares them in a deterministic order.

**Policy ↔ IGP**: Hot-potato routing means IGP cost to next-hop influences BGP exit choice. So BGP policy and IGP metric design are coupled whether you like it or not.

**Policy ↔ Route Reflectors**: RRs can hide alternate paths from clients. That affects policy visibility, MED comparison behavior, and path diversity. ADD-PATH and PIC can reduce these problems.

**Policy ↔ DDoS Controls**: RTBH and FlowSpec use BGP as a signaling plane for security actions. Same protocol, very different intent.

## Edge Cases & Gotchas

**1. AS_PATH prepending is advisory, not authority**: Remote LOCAL_PREF beats your prepends every day of the week. If the upstream sets customer routes to high local-pref, your prepends may do absolutely nothing.

**2. More-specifics override everything**: Longest match beats attribute tuning. If you leak a /24 to solve a TE problem, that /24 becomes the truth whether or not your AS_PATH and MED story says otherwise.

**3. Community taxonomies rot without discipline**: If communities aren't documented, namespaced, and kept consistent, they become numerology. Six months later nobody remembers whether `64512:300:40` means west region de-preference or blackhole on Tuesdays.

**4. Route maps with implicit defaults bite people constantly**: A route policy missing an explicit final reject/accept can behave very differently than expected, especially when moved between vendors or reused in another context.

**5. RTBH can blackhole the wrong thing very efficiently**: That's the whole point. Bad match logic or sloppy automation turns a mitigation tool into a self-DDoS launcher.

**6. Multipath isn't automatic just because two routes look equal in `show bgp`**: Vendor-specific equality checks, arrival order, RR path hiding, and next-hop metric differences all matter.
