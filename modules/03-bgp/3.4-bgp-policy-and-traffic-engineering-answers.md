# 3.4 — BGP Policy & Traffic Engineering — Answer Key

## Question 1
> You're a dual-homed SP with Transit A (AS 3356) and Transit B (AS 174). 70% of your inbound traffic should arrive via Transit A. Design a complete inbound TE solution using at least three different mechanisms. Explain why you'd layer them.

### Answer

Inbound TE is the harder direction — you're trying to influence how OTHER networks send traffic TO you. You don't control their routing decisions. No single mechanism is sufficient because different upstream networks respond to different signals. Layering gives you coverage.

**Mechanism 1: AS_PATH prepending on Transit B.**

Prepend your ASN 2-3 times on announcements to Transit B. This makes your prefixes appear "further away" via Transit B from the perspective of remote ASes running default best path selection. Most of the internet will prefer the shorter AS_PATH through Transit A.

```
IOS-XR (Transit B outbound policy):
  route-policy TRANSIT-B-OUT
    set as-path prepend 65000 65000
  end-policy

Junos:
  policy-statement TRANSIT-B-OUT {
    then {
      as-path-prepend "65000 65000";
    }
  }
```

**Why layer beyond this:** Prepending is a blunt instrument. Some networks ignore AS_PATH length (they use LOCAL_PREF based on business relationships). Others have hardcoded preferences for specific transit providers. Prepending alone typically shifts 40-60% of traffic, not 70%.

**Mechanism 2: Selective prefix announcement (more-specifics via Transit A).**

Announce your aggregate (e.g., /20) to both transits. Additionally, announce more-specific disaggregates (e.g., two /21s) to Transit A only. Remote networks will prefer the more-specific route (longest prefix match in FIB) and send traffic via Transit A for those prefixes.

```
IOS-XR:
  route-policy TRANSIT-A-OUT
    if destination in PREFIX-DISAGGREGATES then
      pass
    endif
    if destination in PREFIX-AGGREGATES then
      pass
    endif
  end-policy

  route-policy TRANSIT-B-OUT
    if destination in PREFIX-AGGREGATES then
      set as-path prepend 65000 65000
      pass
    endif
  end-policy
```

**Why this adds value:** Longest-prefix-match is the one BGP behavior that EVERY router on the internet follows unconditionally. It's the most reliable inbound TE mechanism. By advertising /21s only via Transit A, those /21s will ALWAYS be preferred over the /20 via Transit B.

**Mechanism 3: BGP communities to influence Transit A and Transit B's policies.**

Major transit providers (Lumen/3356, Cogent/174) support BGP communities that let you control how they propagate your routes. Use Transit B's "lower LOCAL_PREF" community to make your routes less preferred within Transit B's network, and use Transit A's "preferred" community if available.

```
Example (real Lumen communities):
  3356:70   — set LOCAL_PREF to 70 within AS 3356 (lower = less preferred)
  3356:90   — set LOCAL_PREF to 90 within AS 3356

Example (real Cogent communities):
  174:990   — prepend to all peers
  174:70    — lower LOCAL_PREF within Cogent
```

Apply Transit B's "lower preference" communities to routes advertised to Transit B.

**Why layer this third mechanism:** Communities influence the internal decisions of your transit providers. Prepending only affects AS_PATH — if a remote network's LOCAL_PREF already favors Transit B, prepending won't help. But if Transit B itself deprioritizes your routes internally (via the community-triggered LOCAL_PREF reduction), their peers and customers are less likely to receive your route via Transit B at all.

**Monitoring:** After deploying all three mechanisms, use `bgp.tools`, `RIPE RIS`, or `RouteViews` to check how your prefixes are seen from various vantage points worldwide. Adjust prepend count and more-specific announcements until you hit the 70/30 target.

## Question 2
> A customer wants RTBH capability — they want to trigger blackholes for their own /32s during DDoS attacks. Design the BGP community scheme, route-map logic, and security controls (how do you prevent them from blackholing someone else's IP?).

### Answer

**Community scheme:**

```
65000:666  — Trigger RTBH (blackhole the destination prefix)
```

The customer announces a /32 host route with community 65000:666 via their eBGP session. Your network installs a route to Null0/discard for that /32, dropping all traffic destined to the attacked IP at your edge — before it consumes bandwidth.

**Route-map / policy logic (IOS-XR):**

```cisco
! Customer inbound policy
route-policy CUSTOMER-RTBH-IN
  if community matches-any RTBH-COMMUNITY then
    ! Only accept /32s for RTBH — reject anything less specific
    if destination in CUSTOMER-PREFIXES-32 then
      set next-hop 192.0.2.1              ! RFC 5737 discard next-hop
      set local-preference 200             ! Ensure blackhole wins over real route
      set community (65000:666) additive
      set origin igp
      pass
    else
      drop                                 ! Reject non-/32 RTBH attempts
    endif
  else
    ! Normal customer route processing
    if destination in CUSTOMER-PREFIXES then
      set local-preference 150
      set community (65000:1100) additive
      pass
    else
      drop
    endif
  endif
end-policy

! On every PE/core router — static route to discard
router static
 address-family ipv4 unicast
  192.0.2.1/32 Null0
```

**Junos equivalent:**
```junos
policy-statement CUSTOMER-RTBH-IN {
    term blackhole {
        from {
            community RTBH;
            prefix-list CUSTOMER-PREFIXES-32;
            route-filter 0.0.0.0/0 prefix-length-range /32-/32;
        }
        then {
            next-hop 192.0.2.1;
            local-preference 200;
            community add RTBH;
            accept;
        }
    }
    term blackhole-reject {
        from community RTBH;
        then reject;                   /* RTBH for non-/32 = denied */
    }
    term normal {
        from prefix-list CUSTOMER-PREFIXES;
        then {
            local-preference 150;
            accept;
        }
    }
    then reject;
}
```

**Security controls — preventing abuse:**

1. **Strict prefix validation.** The `/32` must fall WITHIN the customer's assigned prefix range. Build `CUSTOMER-PREFIXES-32` as a prefix-list containing only /32s within the customer's allocated block (e.g., if customer owns 203.0.113.0/24, allow /32s from 203.0.113.0-203.0.113.255 only). This prevents them from blackholing someone else's IP.

2. **Only accept /32s for RTBH.** Never allow a customer to blackhole a /24 or larger — that could take out an entire subnet. RTBH is for surgical, per-host mitigation.

3. **Max-prefix limit on the RTBH session.** Set a reasonable cap (e.g., 100 /32 RTBH routes). A customer triggering blackholes for 10,000 IPs is either under catastrophic attack (and needs a different solution) or misconfigured.

4. **Separate BGP session for RTBH (optional but recommended).** Run the RTBH signaling on a dedicated eBGP session with its own prefix-list. This separates normal routing from blackhole signaling and makes it easy to tear down RTBH without affecting the customer's normal routes.

5. **Logging and alerting.** Log every RTBH activation. Alert the NOC when a customer triggers a blackhole so operators can verify it's intentional.

6. **Propagation control.** Decide whether to propagate the blackhole to your upstreams (via community 65000:666 → upstream RTBH community). Propagating upstream stops the DDoS traffic before it enters your network — better for your bandwidth. But it gives customers indirect control over upstream routing, so validate carefully.

## Question 3
> Explain the difference between `NO_EXPORT`, `NO_ADVERTISE`, and `NOPEER`. Give a concrete operational scenario where each is the correct choice.

### Answer

All three are well-known BGP communities (RFC 1997) that control route propagation. They differ in scope:

**`NO_EXPORT` (65535:65281):** Do not advertise this route to any eBGP peer. The route stays within the AS (or confederation sub-AS). It CAN be advertised to iBGP peers and confederation eBGP peers, but NOT to true external eBGP peers.

**`NO_ADVERTISE` (65535:65282):** Do not advertise this route to ANY peer — iBGP or eBGP. The route is strictly local to the receiving router. It cannot be propagated to anyone.

**`NOPEER` (65535:65284, RFC 3765):** Do not advertise this route to bilateral eBGP peers (PNI/settlement-free peers). The route CAN still be advertised to transit providers and customers. This is a more nuanced signal than NO_EXPORT — it says "this route is for transit relationships, not peering."

**Scenario for NO_EXPORT:**

You're an SP receiving a more-specific /24 from a customer for traffic engineering purposes. The customer wants traffic from within your network to prefer this /24, but they don't want you to leak the /24 to your upstream transit providers (who should only see the customer's aggregate /20).

```
Customer announces: 203.0.113.0/24 with NO_EXPORT
Customer also announces: 203.0.113.0/20 (no community — advertise normally)
```

Your network installs the /24 for internal longest-prefix-match. Your edge routers see NO_EXPORT and don't advertise the /24 to transit peers. The /20 propagates globally. Result: traffic within your network is steered by the /24, but the internet only sees the /20.

**Scenario for NO_ADVERTISE:**

You need to install a static-like route on a specific router via BGP for policy purposes, but it must never be propagated to any other router. Example: you inject a /32 RTBH route on a single PE to blackhole traffic locally during debugging. You don't want this blackhole to propagate to other PEs (which would blackhole the prefix network-wide).

```
Static → BGP redistribution with NO_ADVERTISE community
```

The blackhole only affects the one router. Remove the static when done.

**Scenario for NOPEER:**

You're a content provider with a hybrid peering strategy. You have transit (paid), PNI peers (settlement-free), and your own customer cone. You want your prefixes reachable globally via transit, and you want your customers to reach you directly. But you DON'T want certain internal prefixes (e.g., your CDN cache IPs) to be visible to settlement-free peers — you only want those peers to access your content via your dedicated peering interconnects, not via transit re-advertisement.

```
CDN cache prefixes: advertise with NOPEER
Public service prefixes: advertise normally
```

Your transit providers propagate the CDN prefixes (NOPEER doesn't block transit). Your peering partners don't receive them directly via PNI sessions. This forces peering traffic to flow through your transit interconnects unless the peer has a dedicated peering arrangement that overrides NOPEER.

**Practical note:** NOPEER is the least commonly used of the three. Many networks ignore it. NO_EXPORT is by far the most operationally important — it's used daily in SP traffic engineering.
