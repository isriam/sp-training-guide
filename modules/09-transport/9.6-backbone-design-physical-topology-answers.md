# 9.6 — Backbone Design & Physical Topology — Answer Key

---

## Question 1
> **Your SP has a 6-node backbone. Finance asks why you can't just build a hub-and-spoke topology centered on your largest PoP instead of the partial mesh design you've proposed. Explain the technical risks and quantify the resilience difference.**

### Answer

Hub-and-spoke has one critical flaw: the hub is a single point of failure that takes down the entire network.

**Resilience comparison:**

A 6-node hub-and-spoke has 5 links (all to the hub). Any spoke link failure isolates one node — acceptable. But the hub node failure isolates ALL 5 spoke nodes from each other simultaneously. There's no recovery path. Your entire backbone is down.

A 6-node partial mesh (designed correctly) has 8–10 links. Each node has at least 2 connections to distinct neighbors. Any single node failure — including your largest PoP — leaves all other nodes reachable via alternate paths. Any single link failure is handled by rerouting through alternate paths with sub-second convergence (TI-LFA or RSVP-TE FRR).

**Quantification:** Hub-and-spoke availability for end-to-end connectivity between any two spoke nodes depends entirely on the hub. If hub availability is 99.99% (52 min downtime/year), then ALL spoke-to-spoke paths have at best 99.99% availability — and the hub is carrying ALL traffic, making it the most failure-prone node due to load. In a partial mesh, end-to-end availability is the product of path availabilities, but with alternate paths, the composite availability is much higher — typically 99.999%+ for dual-connected nodes.

**Traffic engineering:** In hub-and-spoke, ALL traffic between any two spokes takes 2 hops through the hub, even if two spokes are in adjacent cities. This doubles latency for those flows and makes the hub a bandwidth bottleneck. In a partial mesh, traffic between adjacent nodes takes 1 hop on a direct link.

**The financial argument back to finance:** The partial mesh costs more in fiber routes (8-10 vs 5), but the hub in a hub-and-spoke design must be sized to carry ALL backbone traffic — requiring the most expensive, highest-capacity equipment. When you factor in the hub equipment cost, the delta is smaller than it appears. And when you price in the revenue impact of a hub failure taking down the entire backbone, partial mesh is dramatically cheaper on a risk-adjusted basis.

## Question 2
> **You discover that two IP/MPLS links between cities A→B and A→C — which your IGP treats as independent equal-cost paths — both ride wavelengths on the same DWDM system over the same fiber in the same conduit for 200 km. Describe the risk, explain how to fix it at both the physical and protocol layers, and identify the relevant RFCs.**

### Answer

**The risk:** Your IGP (OSPF or IS-IS) sees two independent paths: A→B and A→C. If traffic to destination D is ECMP-balanced across both paths, a single conduit cut on that shared 200 km segment takes out BOTH paths simultaneously. TI-LFA or FRR has computed backup paths assuming these links fail independently — but they don't. Your "protected" traffic has no working path.

**Physical layer fix:** Route the A→C wavelength over a physically diverse fiber path that doesn't share the conduit with A→B. This may mean leasing fiber from a different provider, using a different ROW, or building new conduit. If no diverse physical path exists for the full route, at minimum ensure diversity on the shared 200 km segment.

**Protocol layer fix — SRLG assignment and advertisement:**

1. Assign an SRLG value (say, SRLG 100) to both the A→B and A→C links for the shared conduit segment
2. Advertise SRLG values in the IGP:
   - **OSPF-TE:** RFC 4203, Section 1.3 — SRLG sub-TLV in the Link TLV of TE LSAs (type 1 Opaque LSA)
   - **IS-IS TE:** RFC 5307, Section 1.3 — SRLG TLV (type 138) carried in IS-IS Link State PDUs
3. Configure SRLG-aware path computation:
   - **RSVP-TE:** Primary LSP uses link A→B. Backup LSP is computed by CSPF with constraint "exclude SRLG 100," ensuring it avoids A→C (RFC 4090 for FRR, RFC 4202 for SRLG definition)
   - **SR-TE with PCE:** PCE computes disjoint path pairs with SRLG exclusion as a constraint (RFC 5440 for PCEP, RFC 8664 for SR-PCEP extensions)
   - **TI-LFA:** Configure SRLG-aware backup computation so the post-convergence backup for link A→B doesn't use link A→C

**Relevant RFCs:**
- RFC 4202 — SRLG definition and routing extensions for GMPLS
- RFC 4203 — OSPF extensions for GMPLS (SRLG advertisement)
- RFC 5307 — IS-IS extensions for GMPLS (SRLG advertisement)
- RFC 4090 — Fast Reroute Extensions to RSVP-TE
- RFC 5440 — Path Computation Element (PCE) Communication Protocol
- RFC 8664 — PCEP Extensions for Segment Routing

## Question 3
> **A backbone link is running at 65% peak utilization. Your capacity planning team says this is fine because it's "below 80%." Explain why this is dangerous and calculate the post-failure utilization if this link must absorb traffic from a failed parallel link that was also running at 65%.**

### Answer

65% is not fine. It's dangerous because it ignores failure reroute.

**The problem:** When a parallel link fails, its traffic reroutes onto surviving links. If this link is the primary reroute path for its parallel, it must absorb that traffic.

**Calculation:** If the parallel link was also at 65% utilization, and this link must absorb all of its rerouted traffic:

Post-failure utilization = 65% (existing) + 65% (rerouted) = 130%

130% utilization means you're dropping packets. Not degraded — dropping. You've turned a single link failure into a congestion-induced outage affecting ALL traffic on the surviving link, including traffic that was never on the failed link.

**Even if traffic distributes across multiple surviving paths:** Say you have 3 parallel links, each at 65%. One fails. Its traffic splits across the 2 survivors:

Post-failure utilization per survivor = 65% + (65% / 2) = 65% + 32.5% = 97.5%

Still above 80%, meaning QoS queuing will start tail-dropping or WRED-dropping lower-priority traffic. You'll see increased latency and jitter on everything.

**The correct approach:** With 3 parallel links and a post-failure target of 80%, maximum per-link utilization is:

max_util = (K-1)/K × target = (2/3) × 80% ≈ 53%

With 2 parallel links: (1/2) × 80% = 40%

**What to tell the capacity planning team:** "Below 80%" is a meaningless threshold without context. The question is never "what's the utilization now?" — it's "what's the utilization after the worst-case single failure?" That's the number that must be below 80%.

## Question 4
> **Your C-band DWDM system on a 1,200 km backbone route has 80 channels at 50 GHz fixed grid, of which 72 are lit with 200G DP-QPSK wavelengths. You need to add 2.4 Tbps of capacity. Evaluate three options.**

### Answer

Current: 72 × 200G = 14.4 Tbps. Need: +2.4 Tbps (total 16.8 Tbps). 2.4 Tbps = 12 additional 200G wavelengths.

**Option (a): Fill the remaining 8 channels with 200G DP-QPSK**

- Provides: 8 × 200G = 1.6 Tbps — **not enough**. You need 2.4 Tbps.
- Reach: DP-QPSK at 200G reaches 3,000–6,000 km. At 1,200 km, no issue.
- Pros: Lowest cost (transponders only), fastest deployment (2-4 weeks).
- Cons: Only gets you 1.6 of the 2.4 Tbps needed. You'd be at 80/80 channels — completely full with no headroom for future growth.
- **Verdict: Insufficient.** Gets you 67% of the capacity needed and leaves zero room for growth.

**Option (b): Upgrade existing channels to 400G DP-16QAM**

- Provides: Each upgraded channel goes from 200G to 400G, adding 200G per upgrade. Need 2.4 Tbps / 200G = 12 channel upgrades.
- Reach: DP-16QAM at 400G has typical reach of 500–1,500 km. At 1,200 km, you're at the edge of feasibility. This critically depends on fiber quality, span losses, amplifier noise figures, and whether Raman amplification is available.
- Risk: At 1,200 km, DP-16QAM is marginal. You may need to run link budget analysis for each span. If the route has any high-loss spans (old fiber, long spans, high splice counts), some channels may not close at 400G without regeneration — which defeats the purpose.
- Pros: No new DWDM infrastructure. Transponder swap only.
- Cons: Marginal reach — high risk of not closing on all channels. If 400G channels need wider spectral slots (75 GHz flex-grid), you can't fit as many on the 50 GHz fixed grid — you'd need to rebuild as flex-grid. Also, 400G channels may require different per-channel power, requiring system rebalancing.
- **Verdict: Risky at 1,200 km.** Needs detailed engineering study. Probabilistic constellation shaping (PCS) could help — shaped 16QAM can trade ~10% capacity for significant reach extension, giving ~360G at 1,200 km with better margin.

**Option (c): Deploy L-band**

- Provides: L-band approximately doubles the available spectrum. With 80+ additional channels at 200G DP-QPSK, you get 16+ Tbps of new capacity — far more than the 2.4 Tbps needed.
- Reach: DP-QPSK at 200G in L-band reaches 2,500–5,000 km (slightly reduced vs C-band due to higher EDFA noise figure in L-band). At 1,200 km, no issue.
- Pros: Massive headroom for future growth. Uses proven 200G DP-QPSK modulation with comfortable reach margin. You only need to light 12 of the ~80 new channels to meet current demand.
- Cons: Requires new L-band amplifiers at every amplifier site along the 1,200 km route — significant capex. New L-band ROADM modules at terminal sites. Longer deployment timeline (3-6 months). The step function cost is high even though you only need 12 channels initially.
- **Verdict: Best long-term option** if traffic growth projections justify it. The $X million in L-band infrastructure is amortized over 80+ channels. If you'll need more capacity in 12-24 months anyway, deploy L-band now.

**Recommendation:** Fill the remaining 8 C-band channels (Option a) for immediate relief (+1.6 Tbps), then deploy L-band (Option c) to address the remaining 0.8 Tbps gap and provide growth headroom. Option b is too risky at 1,200 km without detailed link budget analysis and potential Raman upgrades.

## Question 5
> **Explain what an SRLG is, how it's represented in the IGP, and describe a scenario where SRLG-unaware fast reroute would fail to protect traffic.**

### Answer

**What an SRLG is:** A Shared Risk Link Group is a set of logical links that share a common physical resource — a fiber conduit, a bridge, a building, a right-of-way segment. If that physical resource fails, ALL links in the SRLG fail simultaneously. Defined in RFC 4202, each SRLG is identified by a 32-bit unsigned integer.

**IGP representation:**

- **OSPF-TE (RFC 4203):** SRLGs are advertised in the Link TLV (type 2) of Traffic Engineering LSAs (type 10 Opaque LSAs). The Shared Risk Link Group sub-TLV (type 16) contains a list of 32-bit SRLG values associated with the link.

- **IS-IS TE (RFC 5307):** SRLGs are carried in a dedicated TLV (type 138) within Extended IS Reachability TLV (type 22). Each SRLG TLV contains the neighbor system ID, link local/remote identifiers, and a list of 32-bit SRLG values.

Both encodings allow multiple SRLG values per link (a single link may share risk with multiple physical resources — e.g., a fiber that crosses two bridges has two SRLGs).

**Failure scenario:**

```
Topology:
    A ──Link1──▶ B ──Link3──▶ D
    │                         ▲
    └──Link2──▶ C ──Link4────┘

Physical reality:
    Link1 (A→B) and Link2 (A→C) share SRLG 100 (same conduit leaving Site A)
    Link3 (B→D) and Link4 (C→D) are physically diverse
```

Without SRLG awareness, RSVP-TE FRR (RFC 4090) computes a backup for the primary path A→B→D. The facility backup (next-next-hop) for Link1 failure would be A→C→D (via Link2→Link4). This looks correct — Link2 is a different link than Link1.

But Link1 and Link2 share SRLG 100. A conduit cut at Site A's egress takes out BOTH Link1 AND Link2. The backup path A→C→D fails simultaneously with the primary because Link2 is dead. Traffic is unprotected.

With SRLG-aware FRR, the backup computation for Link1 would exclude all links sharing SRLG 100 — meaning Link2 is excluded. If no SRLG-diverse backup exists, the computation fails, alerting the operator that the network has an unprotectable path. This is far better than silently deploying a backup that doesn't actually protect anything.

## Question 6
> **Your backbone route between two cities is 800 km with EDFA amplifiers every 80 km. Estimate the OSNR at the receiver and determine whether 400G DP-16QAM is feasible.**

### Answer

**Given:**
- Route: 800 km, 10 spans of 80 km each
- 9 inline EDFAs (amplifiers between spans) + booster + pre-amp at terminals
- Fiber: G.652, 0.2 dB/km loss
- EDFA noise figure: 5.5 dB
- Required OSNR for 400G DP-16QAM: ~18 dB (at 0.1 nm reference bandwidth)

**Span loss calculation:**
- Per span: 80 km × 0.2 dB/km = 16 dB fiber loss
- Add ~1-2 dB for connectors, splices, and aging margin → ~17-18 dB total span loss

**OSNR estimation (simplified Friis formula for cascaded amplifiers):**

For N identical amplifier stages:

```
OSNR ≈ 58 + P_launch - NF - L_span - 10×log₁₀(N)

Where:
  P_launch = per-channel launch power (typically 0 to +3 dBm) → use +1 dBm
  NF = 5.5 dB
  L_span = 17 dB (including connector/splice margin)
  N = 9 inline amplifiers (for the noise calculation; approximately 10 noise sources including booster/preamp)
```

Using N = 10 noise sources:

```
OSNR ≈ 58 + 1 - 5.5 - 17 - 10×log₁₀(10)
     ≈ 58 + 1 - 5.5 - 17 - 10
     ≈ 26.5 dB
```

**Assessment:** Estimated OSNR of ~26.5 dB vs required ~18 dB for DP-16QAM gives approximately 8.5 dB of margin. However, this simplified calculation doesn't account for:

- Nonlinear penalties (SPM, XPM): reduce effective OSNR by 1–3 dB at high channel loading
- ROADM filtering penalties at terminal nodes: 0.5–1 dB
- Aging margin (fiber, amplifiers): 1–2 dB
- Component variation: 1 dB

**Realistic margin:** 26.5 - 3 (nonlinear) - 1 (ROADM) - 1.5 (aging) - 1 (variation) ≈ 20 dB

At 20 dB vs 18 dB required, you have ~2 dB of margin. **This is tight but feasible** — most optical planners want 3+ dB of margin for production deployment. It would work but with minimal headroom, meaning any fiber degradation, additional channel loading, or amplifier aging could push you below threshold.

**Recommendation:** Feasible for initial deployment but marginal for long-term operation. Consider adding Raman amplification (+3-5 dB OSNR improvement) for comfortable margin, or use probabilistic constellation shaping (PCS) to optimize the capacity-reach tradeoff — shaped 16QAM can achieve ~350-380G with 2-3 dB better reach performance than uniform 16QAM.

## Question 7
> **A junior engineer proposes adding Raman amplification to extend all-optical reach from 1,500 km to 2,500 km. What are the engineering considerations, risks, and prerequisites?**

### Answer

Raman amplification is a legitimate technique that can improve OSNR by 3–6 dB per span, which translates to significant reach extension. But it's not a plug-and-play upgrade.

**Engineering considerations:**

1. **Fiber compatibility:** Raman gain depends on fiber effective area (Aeff) and nonlinear coefficient. Standard G.652 fiber works well. G.654.E (large Aeff) provides less Raman gain per unit power but better overall performance due to lower nonlinear penalties. The existing fiber's Raman gain coefficient must be characterized — you can't assume it from the fiber type alone because aging, splice quality, and manufacturing variations matter.

2. **Pump laser deployment:** Backward-pumped Raman requires high-power pump lasers (typically 300–500 mW per pump at wavelengths ~100 nm below the signal band, e.g., 1430–1470 nm for C-band amplification). These pumps must be installed at EVERY amplifier site along the route — that's potentially 15-25 sites on a 1,500-2,500 km route.

3. **Safety:** Raman pump powers are Class 3B or Class 4 laser hazards. If a fiber is disconnected while pumps are active, you have high-power laser light shooting out of an open connector. Automatic laser shutdown (ALS) is mandatory. Safety procedures for fiber maintenance must be updated.

4. **Double Rayleigh backscatter (DRB):** Raman amplification in the fiber creates backscattered light that can cause multipath interference (MPI). This gets worse with higher Raman gain. Typical practical limit is ~15 dB of on-off Raman gain per span before DRB penalties become significant. Design must verify DRB penalty is acceptable.

5. **Noise transfer from pump lasers:** Relative Intensity Noise (RIN) from pump lasers transfers to the signal. Low-RIN pump lasers are essential. Pump-to-pump interactions in multi-wavelength pump schemes must be modeled.

6. **Power consumption:** Raman pumps at every amplifier site increase per-site power consumption by 50–100W typically. Over 20+ sites, this adds up — and some remote amplifier huts have limited power budgets.

7. **Interaction with existing EDFA settings:** Adding Raman gain changes the input power to the EDFA at each site. EDFA gain and tilt settings must be re-optimized. The entire system needs rebalancing — this is not a change you make on one span in isolation.

**Risks:**
- Incomplete fiber characterization leading to less Raman gain than expected
- Safety incidents during installation or subsequent fiber maintenance
- System rebalancing required during deployment, potentially impacting live traffic
- Increased operational complexity (more components to monitor and maintain)

**Prerequisites:**
- Complete fiber characterization (loss, Raman gain coefficient, PMD) on every span
- Verification that amplifier hut power budgets can support pump modules
- Safety procedure updates and technician training
- System-level simulation of the entire route with Raman to verify OSNR meets target at the desired reach
- Maintenance window planning for EDFA rebalancing during deployment

**Bottom line:** The 1,500→2,500 km reach extension is plausible with Raman (~3-5 dB OSNR improvement). But this is a multi-month engineering project, not a configuration change. Budget 3-6 months for engineering, procurement, and deployment.

## Question 8
> **3 of 4 parallel logical links share a common conduit crossing a river bridge. Describe how you'd redesign the traffic engineering using both RSVP-TE and SR-TE approaches.**

### Answer

**The problem:** You have 4 logical links between PoP-A and PoP-B. Links 1, 2, and 3 share SRLG "River-Bridge-X." Link 4 is on a separate physical path (50 km longer). If the bridge is damaged, 75% of your capacity between these PoPs fails simultaneously.

**RSVP-TE approach:**

1. Assign SRLG value (e.g., SRLG 200) to Links 1, 2, and 3. Link 4 has no SRLG (or a different SRLG for its own physical risks).

2. Advertise SRLG 200 via OSPF-TE (RFC 4203) or IS-IS TE (RFC 5307) so all headend routers know about the shared risk.

3. Design LSP placement:
   - Primary LSPs should be distributed across the SRLG boundary: place no more than 50% of traffic capacity on Links 1/2/3 combined
   - For each primary LSP using Link 1, 2, or 3: configure a backup LSP with SRLG exclusion constraint — CSPF must avoid SRLG 200, forcing backup onto Link 4 or alternate routes through the network
   - If using facility backup (bypass tunnels): the bypass tunnel for Links 1/2/3 must be SRLG-aware and route via Link 4

4. Capacity constraint: Link 4 must have enough capacity to absorb worst-case reroute traffic from Links 1/2/3 simultaneously. If Links 1/2/3 each carry 100G of protected traffic and Link 4 is only 100G, you can only protect 100G — not 300G. Size Link 4 accordingly or accept partial protection.

**SR-TE approach (with PCE):**

1. Same SRLG assignment and IGP advertisement.

2. Deploy a stateful PCE (RFC 5440) with SRLG awareness.

3. The PCE computes globally optimal path pairs. For traffic between PoP-A and PoP-B, the PCE creates:
   - A primary SR-TE policy using Links 1/2/3 (standard path, lower latency)
   - A backup SR-TE policy that explicitly avoids SRLG 200 (routes via Link 4)
   - The PCE can compute these as a synchronized disjoint pair, ensuring no SRLG overlap

4. On-Demand Nexthop (ODN) alternative: Configure the headend with a color/community that signals "SRLG-diverse from primary." The PCE computes the diverse path on demand. The headend doesn't need to know SRLG details — it just expresses intent.

5. Weighted ECMP: Use SR-TE weighted ECMP to distribute traffic ~25% on Links 1, 2, 3, and 25% on Link 4 during normal operation. This ensures Link 4 is carrying live traffic (validating the path) and limits maximum traffic on SRLG 200 to 75%.

**The SR-TE advantage here:** A centralized PCE can globally optimize across multiple LSP/policy pairs simultaneously. In RSVP-TE, each headend computes independently — headend X might grab Link 4 for its backup, leaving headend Y with no SRLG-diverse option. The PCE avoids this resource contention.

## Question 9
> **Can you deliver less than 10 ms one-way latency between City A and City B, 1,600 km apart? Calculate with route factor 1.3, two ROADM nodes (10 µs each), and optionally one regen site (40 µs).**

### Answer

**Fiber route distance:**
- Straight-line: 1,600 km
- Route factor: 1.3
- Actual fiber: 1,600 × 1.3 = 2,080 km

**Propagation delay (fiber only):**
- 2,080 km × 4.9 µs/km = 10,192 µs = **10.192 ms**

The propagation delay alone (10.192 ms) already exceeds the 10 ms budget. Adding equipment delays makes it worse:

**With 2 ROADM pass-through nodes (no regen):**
- 10,192 µs + (2 × 10 µs) = 10,212 µs = **10.212 ms**
- **Exceeds 10 ms budget.** Not achievable.

**With 2 ROADMs + 1 regen site:**
- 10,192 µs + (2 × 10 µs) + 40 µs = 10,252 µs = **10.252 ms**
- **Even worse.** The regen adds 40 µs but the problem was already the propagation delay.

**Analysis:** The 10 ms budget is fundamentally limited by the speed of light. At 4.9 µs/km, 10 ms allows a maximum fiber route of ~2,040 km. With a route factor of 1.3, that means the straight-line distance can't exceed ~1,570 km. At 1,600 km straight-line, you're ~30 km over the physical limit.

**Could it work?**
- If the actual fiber route has a lower route factor (1.25 instead of 1.3): 1,600 × 1.25 = 2,000 km → 9,800 µs = 9.8 ms + 20 µs = 9.82 ms. **Barely feasible** with a straighter route.
- If using newer hollow-core fiber (~3.5 µs/km instead of 4.9 µs/km): 2,080 × 3.5 = 7,280 µs = 7.28 ms. **Easily feasible** but hollow-core fiber is not yet commercially deployed at scale.

**Bottom line:** At 1,600 km with standard fiber, 10 ms one-way is at the ragged edge of physics. You'd need to find or build a fiber route with a route factor below 1.275 — essentially a nearly straight path. This is a scenario where route selection is driven by latency, not cost or ease of construction.

## Question 10
> **Explain N+1 vs N+M redundancy for backbone fiber pairs. With 6 working fiber pairs, how many protect pairs for 99.99% availability?**

### Answer

**N+1 redundancy:** 6 working fiber pairs + 1 protect pair. The protect pair can absorb the traffic from any ONE failed working pair. If working pair #3 fails, its wavelengths are switched to the protect pair. Simple, cost-effective. But if two fiber pairs fail simultaneously (e.g., a conduit contains 2 pairs and is cut), you can only protect one — the other's traffic is dropped.

**N+M redundancy:** 6 working fiber pairs + M protect pairs. Survives M simultaneous fiber pair failures. More expensive but protects against multi-failure scenarios.

**For 99.99% availability:**

The key question is: what failure scenarios must you survive within your downtime budget (52.6 minutes/year)?

- **Single fiber pair failure:** Most common. Caused by backhoe, rodent, equipment failure. MTBF for a fiber pair on a long-haul route is typically 1-3 failures per 1,000 km per year. N+1 handles this.

- **Dual fiber pair failure:** Less common but happens when multiple pairs share a conduit (and they often do — the whole point of sharing conduit is cost). If 4 of your 6 pairs share conduit A and 2 share conduit B, a single backhoe on conduit A takes out 4 pairs simultaneously.

- **Conduit-level failure:** This is the real threat. A single conduit cut can take out 2-6 fiber pairs. If all 6 working pairs are in 2 conduits (3 pairs each), you need to survive a conduit cut — meaning you need 3 protect pairs in a THIRD, physically diverse conduit.

**Recommendation for 99.99%:**

2 protect fiber pairs (N+2), routed in a conduit physically diverse from ALL working pairs.

Rationale:
- N+1 is insufficient because dual-pair failures from shared conduit are not rare enough for 99.99%
- N+2 survives any 2 simultaneous fiber pair failures, covering the conduit-cut scenario where 2 pairs in one conduit are lost
- The protect pairs MUST be in a separate conduit — putting protect pairs in the same conduit as working pairs defeats the purpose
- If working pairs are spread across multiple conduits (good design), N+2 in a diverse conduit covers any single conduit failure plus one additional independent failure

For true 99.99% with catastrophic protection, some SPs deploy N+2 with the protect pairs on a completely separate physical route (different ROW, different cities if necessary). This adds significant cost but provides geo-diverse protection.

## Question 11
> **Metro ring to backbone partial mesh interconnection. Draw the topology and explain diversity considerations.**

### Answer

**Physical topology:**

```
                BACKBONE (Partial Mesh)
    ┌──────── Backbone Node X ────────── Backbone Node Y ──────┐
    │              │                          │                 │
    │              │ (diverse                  │                 │
    │              │  entrance                │                 │
    │              │  facilities)             │                 │
    │         ┌────┴────┐               ┌────┴────┐            │
    │         │  Interco │               │  Interco │            │
    │         │  Node 1  │               │  Node 2  │            │
    │         └────┬────┘               └────┬────┘            │
    │              │                          │                 │
    │              │     METRO RING           │                 │
    │              │                          │                 │
    │         ┌────┴────┐               ┌────┴────┐            │
    │         │ Ring     │───────────────│ Ring     │            │
    │         │ Node A   │               │ Node B   │            │
    │         └────┬────┘               └────┬────┘            │
    │              │                          │                 │
    │         ┌────┴────┐               ┌────┴────┐            │
    │         │ Ring     │───────────────│ Ring     │            │
    │         │ Node C   │               │ Node D   │            │
    │         └─────────┘               └─────────┘            │
    └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Diversity considerations at the junction:**

1. **Dual-homing the ring:** The metro ring must connect to TWO backbone nodes (Backbone Node X and Y) via two separate interconnection nodes (Interco 1 and 2). Single-homing the ring to one backbone node creates a SPOF.

2. **Entrance facility diversity:** The interconnection between metro ring and backbone must use SEPARATE entrance facilities (different building entrances, different conduit entries). If both handoffs enter Backbone Node X through the same fiber vault, a fire in that vault kills both.

3. **Conduit diversity for the ring-to-backbone links:** The fiber from Interco Node 1 to Backbone Node X must be in a different conduit than the fiber from Interco Node 2 to Backbone Node Y. Ideally, different ROW entirely.

4. **SRLG assignment:** Assign SRLGs to the interconnection links. The two ring-to-backbone handoffs should have NO shared SRLGs. Backbone path computation must treat them as independent.

5. **Ring node placement:** Interco Nodes 1 and 2 should be on opposite sides of the ring (not adjacent). This ensures that a ring cut doesn't isolate both interconnection points simultaneously.

6. **Capacity planning:** Each ring-to-backbone handoff must have enough capacity to carry the full ring's backbone-destined traffic alone (in case the other handoff fails). Otherwise, a single handoff failure causes congestion at the surviving handoff.

7. **The building problem:** Many SPs co-locate backbone and metro equipment in the same building. If Backbone Node X and Interco Node 1 are in the same building, a building-level failure (fire, flood, extended power outage) takes out both the backbone node AND the metro interconnection. Ideally, interconnection nodes are in separate buildings from backbone nodes — but practically, this is often not achievable and is mitigated through building-level resiliency (backup power, fire suppression, diverse HVAC).

## Question 12
> **Build new fiber route ($40M, 18 months) vs. add L-band ($8M, 6 months) for a Tier 2 SP facing capacity exhaustion in 12 months at 85% C-band utilization with 40% annual traffic growth.**

### Answer

**Immediate need:** Capacity exhaustion in 12 months. L-band can be deployed in 6 months. New fiber takes 18 months — you'll be exhausted 6 months before it's ready. **L-band first, or you have a service-impacting capacity crisis.**

**5-year capacity analysis with 40% annual growth:**

Current C-band capacity = 100% (call it X Tbps). Currently 85% utilized.

| Year | Traffic (×current) | C-band Only | C-band + L-band | New Fiber (C-band) |
|------|-------------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 0 | 0.85X | 85% utilized | — | — |
| 1 | 1.19X | **119% — EXHAUSTED** | 62% (C+L ≈ 1.9X capacity) | Not available yet |
| 1.5 | 1.31X | — | 69% | New fiber online |
| 2 | 1.67X | — | 88% (approaching exhaustion) | 83% on each (C-band × 2) |
| 3 | 2.34X | — | **123% — EXHAUSTED** | 58% on each |
| 4 | 3.27X | — | — | 82% on each |
| 5 | 4.58X | — | — | **115% — EXHAUSTED** |

**Key observations:**
- L-band alone buys ~2 years (exhaustion at year ~2.5 with C+L)
- New fiber alone buys ~4 years (exhaustion at year ~4.5 with 2× C-band)
- L-band + new fiber buys ~4.5+ years

**5-year TCO comparison:**

*Option 1: L-band only ($8M)*
- Handles years 0–2.5
- At year 2.5, you still need new fiber ($40M) or L-band on a second existing route
- 5-year cost: $8M + $40M (new fiber at year 2) = **$48M**, but new fiber ordered at year 2 with 18-month lead time arrives at year 3.5 — right when you need it
- Risk: Tight timing at year 2.5-3.5 gap

*Option 2: New fiber only ($40M)*
- 6-month capacity crisis before it arrives
- 5-year cost: $40M + interim measures (lease wavelengths? ~$2-5M) = **$42-45M**
- Risk: 6 months without capacity solution. Leasing may not be available.

*Option 3: L-band now + new fiber ordered immediately ($48M)*
- L-band covers the immediate need (6-month deploy)
- New fiber arrives at 18 months with margin
- L-band on original route + C-band on new route provides maximum headroom
- 5-year cost: **$48M**, but with maximum flexibility and zero risk periods

**Recommendation: Option 3 — deploy L-band now AND order new fiber immediately.**

The $8M L-band deployment covers the 12-month crisis with 6 months to spare. The new fiber build starts immediately, arriving at month 18 with ample headroom. Total cost is $48M but eliminates all risk windows and provides the most capacity for years 3-5.

The additional $8M for L-band (vs. waiting for fiber only) is insurance against the 6-month gap. For a Tier 2 SP, the revenue at risk from a capacity crisis (customer churn, SLA penalties, inability to sell new services) almost certainly exceeds $8M.

## Question 13
> **Explain how a PCE provides better SRLG-diverse path computation than distributed headend computation. Give a specific example of distributed failure.**

### Answer

**Why PCE is better for SRLG-diverse path computation:**

A PCE (RFC 5440) has a **global view** of the entire network topology, including all link states, bandwidth availability, and SRLG assignments. It computes paths centrally, considering all existing LSPs/policies. Distributed headend computation, by contrast, operates with local information — each headend independently computes its paths based on the same IGP database but without knowledge of what other headends are computing simultaneously.

**The specific advantage for disjoint pairs:**

Computing SRLG-disjoint path pairs is a **global optimization problem**. The mathematically optimal disjoint pair between nodes A and Z may not be found by independently computing "best primary" then "best backup avoiding primary's SRLGs." This is because the globally optimal pair might use a slightly suboptimal primary to enable a much better backup — something a greedy sequential computation misses.

**Specific example of distributed failure:**

```
Topology:
    A ──L1── B ──L3── D
    │        │        │
    L2       L4       L5
    │        │        │
    E ──L6── C ──L7── F ──L8── Z
    
SRLG assignments:
    L1, L6 share SRLG-α (common conduit segment A-to-midpoint)
    L3, L7 share SRLG-β (common conduit segment midpoint-to-D/F)
    L2 has SRLG-γ
    L4 has SRLG-δ
    L5 has SRLG-ε
    L8 has SRLG-ζ
```

**Headend A wants two SRLG-disjoint paths to Z:**

*Distributed computation (greedy sequential):*
1. Compute shortest primary: A→L1→B→L3→D→L5→F→L8→Z (cost 4, uses SRLGs α, β, ε, ζ)
2. Compute backup excluding SRLGs {α, β, ε, ζ}: Must avoid L1 (α), L6 (α), L3 (β), L7 (β). Available links: L2, L4, L5, L8. Path: A→L2→E→...stuck. E connects to C via L6 (excluded, SRLG α). **No valid backup path found.** The headend reports "no disjoint path available."

*PCE computation (global optimization):*
The PCE considers alternate primary paths:
1. Primary: A→L2→E→L6→C→L7→F→L8→Z (cost 4, uses SRLGs γ, α, β, ζ)
2. Backup excluding {γ, α, β, ζ}: A→L1(α excluded)...this doesn't work either.

Let's try:
1. Primary: A→L1→B→L4→C→L7→F→L8→Z (cost 4, uses SRLGs α, δ, β, ζ)
2. Backup excluding {α, δ, β, ζ}: A→L2→E→L6(α excluded)...still stuck.

Actually, the key point is better illustrated with bandwidth contention:

**Bandwidth contention example:**

```
    A ══L1══ B ══L2══ Z
    │                  │
    L3                L4
    │                  │
    C ══════L5════════ D

All links: 100G capacity
L1, L3 share SRLG-X (same conduit)
L2, L4 share SRLG-Y (same conduit)
```

Two headends (A and C) each want SRLG-disjoint path pairs to Z:

*Distributed (both compute simultaneously):*
- Headend A: Primary A→L1→B→L2→Z. Backup (avoid SRLG-X, SRLG-Y): A→L3(SRLG-X, excluded!)... **no backup.**
  
  Hmm, let's reconsider. A better illustration:

*Headend A computes:* Primary = A→B→Z (via L1, L2). Backup avoiding SRLGs X and Y = A→C→D→Z (via L3, L5, L4). L3 shares SRLG-X with L1 — excluded! No backup via that path.

The fundamental issue: **In RSVP-TE distributed computation, each headend greedily picks its optimal primary, and the "best" primary may make finding an SRLG-diverse backup impossible.** A PCE can recognize this and pick a slightly suboptimal primary (e.g., a longer path) that enables a valid SRLG-diverse backup. It solves the pair jointly rather than sequentially.

Additionally, with multiple headends competing for the same SRLG-diverse backup links, distributed computation creates race conditions — two headends may both compute backups through the same link, exceeding its capacity. The PCE serializes these decisions and accounts for all existing reservations.

## Question 14
> **Verify 45 ms one-way latency for a 9,000 km transpacific submarine cable against theoretical minimum.**

### Answer

**Theoretical minimum (propagation delay only):**

Speed of light in fiber: ~200,000 km/s (c/n where n ≈ 1.468 for standard SMF)
Propagation delay: ~4.9 µs/km

For 9,000 km cable:
- Theoretical minimum = 9,000 × 4.9 µs = 44,100 µs = **44.1 ms**

**Quoted latency: 45 ms**

**Difference: 45 - 44.1 = 0.9 ms (900 µs)**

**Factors accounting for the 0.9 ms difference:**

1. **Cable route vs. straight-line:** Submarine cables don't follow the geodesic. They route around seamounts, avoid areas with seismic activity, follow established cable corridors, and route to cable landing stations that may not be at the ideal geographic point. But the 9,000 km is likely the cable length, not straight-line distance — so this may already be accounted for.

2. **Amplifier (repeater) delays:** Submarine cables have optical amplifiers (EDFAs) every ~50-70 km. For a 9,000 km cable, that's approximately 130-180 repeaters. Each repeater adds a small delay (~1-5 ns for passive EDFA, more for any active components). Total: ~0.2-0.9 µs. **Negligible.**

3. **Shore-end equipment (SLTE):** Submarine Line Terminal Equipment at each end performs signal processing, equalization, and potentially OTN framing. This adds ~50-200 µs per end. Total: ~100-400 µs. **Significant contributor.**

4. **Fiber refractive index variation:** Different submarine fiber types have slightly different refractive indices. The actual propagation speed may be slightly different from the 4.9 µs/km standard estimate. Submarine fiber (G.654.B/C/D) can have slightly different group index than standard G.652.

5. **Chromatic dispersion compensation:** While modern coherent receivers handle CD digitally, the DSP processing adds a small delay (~10-50 µs per terminal). **Minor contributor.**

6. **Cable slack:** Submarine cables have slack built in (excess cable on the seabed) to allow for repairs. Slack adds ~1-5% to the actual light path length vs. the charted cable length. If 9,000 km is the charted length, actual fiber may be 9,100-9,450 km, adding 0.5-2.2 ms.

**Most likely explanation for the 0.9 ms delta:**
- SLTE terminal equipment: ~200-400 µs (both ends)
- Cable slack and route meander: ~200-500 µs  
- DSP processing: ~100-200 µs

The quoted 45 ms is very close to theoretical minimum, which is expected for a modern submarine cable — they're designed for minimum latency, especially on routes serving financial markets.

## Question 15
> **Describe the capacity planning workflow from traffic measurement through upgrade decision. How does it differ for DWDM channel exhaustion vs. ample wavelength headroom?**

### Answer

**Complete capacity planning workflow:**

**Phase 1: Measurement (continuous)**
1. Collect per-link utilization data at 5-minute intervals (SNMP, streaming telemetry, gNMI)
2. Identify **peak utilization** — typically 95th percentile over a rolling 30-day window
3. Record traffic growth rate — compare current peak to same-period peaks from 3, 6, 12, and 24 months ago
4. Build **traffic matrices** — which source-destination pairs contribute what fraction of each link's load
5. Document current DWDM channel utilization: how many channels lit, how many available, what band

**Phase 2: Projection (quarterly)**
1. Extrapolate traffic growth using historical compound rate (e.g., 40% annually = ~2.8% monthly)
2. Apply known step-function events (new customer onboarding, CDN deployment, merger/acquisition traffic)
3. Calculate **exhaustion date** — when peak utilization will exceed post-failure threshold (typically 80% post-failure, meaning ~50% normal operation)
4. Calculate **order date** — exhaustion date minus deployment lead time (weeks for wavelengths, months for fiber)

**Phase 3: Decision (triggered by threshold or timeline)**

| Metric | Threshold | Action |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Peak utilization > 40% (2-path route) | Warning | Begin planning next upgrade |
| Peak utilization > 50% (2-path route) | Order | Procure transponders/fiber |
| Post-failure utilization > 80% | Critical | Emergency capacity add |
| DWDM channels > 70% lit | Warning | Plan L-band or new fiber pair |
| DWDM channels > 85% lit | Order | Procure L-band equipment or new fiber |
| Time-to-exhaustion < deployment lead time | Critical | Expedite or implement interim measures |

**Phase 4: Upgrade execution**
1. Engineering: Link budget, wavelength plan, SRLG verification
2. Procurement: Transponders, amplifiers, fiber (as needed)
3. Deployment: Install, test, turn up with traffic migration plan
4. Validation: Verify performance, update monitoring baselines

---

**How the workflow differs based on DWDM headroom:**

**Scenario A: Ample wavelength headroom (system < 70% channel utilization)**

- Upgrade is incremental: add transponders at each end, configure the wavelength on existing ROADMs
- Lead time: 2–4 weeks (procurement) + 1 day (deployment)
- No impact to existing services — new channels are added to the existing DWDM system
- Cost: Per-wavelength cost only (transponder pair: $10K-$80K depending on capacity)
- Planning horizon: 6–12 months ahead is sufficient
- Decision criteria: Simple utilization threshold triggers transponder order

**Scenario B: Approaching DWDM channel exhaustion (system > 80% channel utilization)**

The workflow changes fundamentally:

1. **Lead times explode:** Adding L-band requires new amplifier modules at every site (3-6 month project). New fiber pairs require new DWDM line systems (6-12 months). New fiber routes require construction (12-36 months).

2. **Step-function costs:** You can't add "one more wavelength." You must deploy an entire L-band system or an entire new DWDM line system on a new fiber pair. The cost goes from $50K (one transponder) to $2-10M (L-band across a route).

3. **Planning horizon extends:** Must plan 18-36 months ahead because the lead time for the capacity upgrade exceeds the time until exhaustion if you wait.

4. **Interim measures may be needed:** If planning wasn't done early enough, you may need to lease wavelengths from another carrier, implement aggressive traffic engineering (move traffic to less-loaded routes), or compress/groom existing traffic to buy time until the major upgrade is complete.

5. **Amplifier impact analysis required:** On a near-full system, adding channels may require rebalancing amplifier gains, which can briefly impact existing channels. This requires maintenance windows and careful change management — unlike the "just light another lambda" simplicity of a system with headroom.

6. **Technology refresh opportunity:** If the system is old enough to be near channel exhaustion, it may make sense to upgrade to newer coherent technology (e.g., replace 100G with 400G channels) rather than adding more channels. This "grow by replacing" approach can double or quadruple capacity without adding channels — but requires transponder swaps on live traffic.

**The meta-lesson:** Capacity planning gets exponentially harder as you approach DWDM system limits. The time to plan for channel exhaustion is when you're at 50-60% utilization, not 85%. By 85%, your options are constrained and your timelines are dangerously tight.
