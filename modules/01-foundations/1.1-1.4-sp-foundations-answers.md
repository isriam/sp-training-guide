# 1.1-1.4 — SP Foundations — Answer Key

These questions are IE-SP caliber — scenario-based, requiring you to apply multiple concepts.

---

## Question 1
> A regional ISP has 800 routers in a single IS-IS Level 2 domain with 45,000 internal prefixes. During a backbone P-router software upgrade, the NOC observes BGP VPN reconvergence taking 4–6 minutes for some customer VRFs. IS-IS reconvergence itself completes in 400ms. What is the most likely cause, and what design change would address it?

### Answer

BGP reconvergence is separated from IGP reconvergence. The IGP reconverges in 400ms (fast), but BGP VPN routes need to be individually reconverged when the BGP next-hop changes. Without PIC (Prefix Independent Convergence), each of the thousands of VPN prefixes pointing to the affected next-hop must be individually re-resolved — with 45K prefixes and RR best-path recalculation, this takes minutes. Solutions: (a) enable ADD-PATH so PIC can pre-install backup VPN routes (the backup next-hop is already in the FIB — switchover is immediate), (b) scale-up or add redundant RRs to reduce recalculation time, (c) use BGP GR (Graceful Restart) on the router being upgraded so BGP sessions don't drop in the first place. The IS-IS reconvergence being fast rules out IGP as the bottleneck — this is purely a BGP design issue. Note: on Junos, BGP route resolution is event-driven rather than timer-based; on IOS-XR, the BGP scanner interval (default 60s) adds additional delay.

## Question 2
> You are designing a national carrier backbone for a new SP. The sales team is committing 99.999% uptime SLAs to enterprise customers. What is your failure recovery strategy, and specifically how do you justify the choice between RSVP-TE FRR and TI-LFA?

### Answer

99.999% = 5.26 minutes/year. You must achieve sub-50ms recovery for all backbone failures. Both RSVP-TE FRR and TI-LFA can meet the 50ms target. The choice: RSVP-TE FRR requires pre-signaled bypass LSPs. In a full-mesh PE-to-PE design, each P router transits O(N²) LSPs (where N = number of PEs), plus bypass tunnels for FRR. While modern platforms (ASR 9000, MX series) handle 50K–100K+ RSVP-TE LSPs, the operational complexity of managing this state — Path/Resv refresh, make-before-break, bandwidth bookkeeping — becomes the real scaling constraint. TI-LFA uses SR-MPLS with no backbone soft-state — backup paths computed locally per-prefix, no signaling overhead, linear scaling. For a greenfield national carrier, TI-LFA + SR-MPLS is the correct choice: less state, simpler operations, equivalent convergence, better scale. RSVP-FRR is retained only for legacy MPLS-TE deployments or specific use cases requiring bandwidth guarantees on protected paths.

## Question 3
> A customer reports intermittent traffic blackholing in their L3VPN. The PE serving the customer has the VPN routes in the VRF. The remote PE also has the routes. Traffic flows from Site A to Site B but not from Site B to Site A. What is your diagnostic approach?

### Answer

Asymmetric blackhole in L3VPN points to an asymmetry in one of: (a) the MPLS label path (Site B's PE can't reach Site A's PE label), (b) the VPNv4 route on Site B's PE has a BGP next-hop that isn't MPLS-reachable, or (c) there's a policy discrepancy (Route Target not imported on one side, or prefix filtered at CE). Diagnostic steps on Site B's PE: verify the VPNv4 route exists (Junos: `show route table bgp.l3vpn.0`; IOS-XR: `show bgp vpnv4 unicast`); check if it's installed in the VRF (Junos: `show route table VRF-NAME`; IOS-XR: `show route vrf VRF-NAME`); verify the transport LSP to Site A's PE loopback exists (Junos: `show mpls lsp`; IOS-XR: `show mpls forwarding`); trace the MPLS path (Junos: `traceroute mpls segment-routing`; IOS-XR: `traceroute mpls ipv4 [site-A-PE-loopback]`). The asymmetry often means the issue is only in the B→A direction at the MPLS transport level — a broken IGP route to Site A's PE loopback on the backbone, or a missing/withdrawn VPN label on one direction.

## Question 4
> Your NOC receives an alarm: a backbone P router in the New York core has gone offline. Within 100ms, all backbone traffic around the failed router has reconverged via TI-LFA backup paths. Two minutes later, 15% of BGP VPN routes still show as unreachable on some PEs. What is happening and how do you resolve it?

### Answer

TI-LFA handles the fast-path (sub-50ms switchover to backup). But the 15% of VPN routes still showing unreachable after 2 minutes indicates a BGP-layer issue. The most likely cause: some PE loopbacks whose shortest MPLS path traversed the failed P router now have a new (longer) path after IGP reconvergence. VPN routes whose BGP next-hop points to those PE loopbacks need the PE to resolve the new MPLS path. Without PIC, each affected VPN prefix must be individually reconverged — with thousands of prefixes, this takes minutes. Check: `show bgp vpnv4 unicast` for routes with unreachable or changed next-hops; verify IGP reachability to all PE loopbacks; confirm MPLS labels are installed for the new paths. Resolution: enable PIC + ADD-PATH so backup VPN paths are pre-installed. For planned maintenance, use IS-IS overload bit to drain traffic before the P router goes offline.

## Question 5
> You are reviewing a proposed SP backbone design that uses OSPF for the IGP instead of IS-IS. The designer argues this is fine because both protocols support SR-MPLS and TI-LFA. What is your response?

### Answer

The designer is technically correct that OSPF supports SR-MPLS and TI-LFA. The choice is not about protocol capability. Arguments for IS-IS over OSPF in SP context: (1) IS-IS has a richer operational history in SP environments — vendor implementations are more mature for SP-specific extensions like SR, BGP-LS, and large-scale deployment; (2) IS-IS L1/L2 hierarchy is cleaner for multi-area SP design than OSPF's multi-area (no stub area ambiguity, no Type 3/4/5 LSA flooding complexity); (3) IS-IS runs over CLNS which historically made it more flexible for non-IP links; (4) the industry conventions, the IE-SP exam expectations, and the interoperability testing were built around IS-IS for SP. If the SP is running a purely homogeneous Cisco IOS-XR network and the designer prefers OSPF, it's defensible. In a multi-vendor environment or greenfield national carrier, IS-IS is the correct choice.
